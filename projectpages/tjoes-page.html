OBJECTIVE AND INSPIRATION

WHAT:

A case study and redesign of the Trader Joe’s website for a new, unexpected audience

WHY:

Learning how to analyze an existing website and its target audience, identifying strengths, opportunities, weaknesses, and threats as well as how to design and prototype a website with a new and different audience in mind.

When considering the accessibility of a grocery store today, considerations can and ought to venture beyond the physical aspects of the store. The website needs to be set up in a way that is easy for users to read, comprehend, and navigate, but accessibility features can go beyond that as well. In a world full of delivery subscription box services and Instacart almost anything can be ordered online and delivered to a user’s front door. Trader Joe’s, however, remains an exception. This project explores a user’s navigation through the Trader Joe’s website, examining how visual information, color, and layout can influence one’s ability to find and acquire what they need as they attempt to browse recipes and add items to their cart. This project also imagines and explores how the addition of online orders could make a difference in terms of accessibility.

This case study was conducted through competitor research, user persona development, SWOT analysis, user flow sketching, the creation of mobile, desktop, and tablet wireframes as well as prototypes.

The audience being considered for this project includes individuals who might normally fall under the Trader Joe’s target audience but are unable to visit the brick and mortar store. This includes individuals living in areas where Trader Joe’s doesn’t have stores, such as small towns and rural areas, individuals who are unable to shop in person due to disabilities, physical or mental health concerns, as well as individuals without the time affluence necessary to accommodate in-person shopping trips. This audience is meant to include individuals who have different sensory needs due to neurodivergence or anxiety who may become overstimulated by the lighting and busy environment of the store. This is reflected in the considerations I took when evaluating the amount of visual input to include as well as what type of color palette to use in my redesign. Everyone included in this audience would benefit from access to affordable, healthy food and would need to order Trader Joe’s products for delivery or pickup. PROCESS | RESEARCH AND WIREFRAMES

After conducting research to determine the strengths, opportunities, weaknesses, and threats as well as evaluating the existing user persona, new user personas were created to represent the unexpected audience the Trader Joe’s Website would be redesigned for.

Once research had been done and new user personas had been created, wireframes were created for mobile, tablet, and desktop designs.

After creating the wireframes, I was ready to get to work prototyping in Figma.

OUTCOME | SOLUTIONS AND PROTOTYPES

Buttons were created to be large, appealing looking, and accessible. Navigational choices and buttons have been limited to the minimum necessary on each page to avoid overwhelm or decision fatigue. The user flow has been designed with the same intentions, guiding the user through options that match their needs clearly and concisely while also being aesthetically pleasing.

To solve the obstacle of the unexpected audience not being able to access the store in person, three new features were introduced : weekly meal kit subscriptions, the ability to order groceries for delivery, and the ability to order groceries for pickup. If users do not wish to subscribe for weekly meal kits but do want to give a featured meal kit recipe a try, they are able to click a button to add meal kit ingredients to their grocery order without subscribing. This solves the issue Trader Joe’s currently has with products in the recipes not being linked to the products on the website. The “add to shopping list” feature from the existing Trader Joe’s website has been replaced with the option to add items to a cart and actually check out.

Product imagery is used to support text and help users to identify items, while simultaneously adding to the visual representation of the brand. A hero banner was added to the home page featuring clear photography of various products with the Trader Joe’s logo over it. This imagery helps users associate their online experience with the feeling of picking out groceries, specifically at Trader Joe’s. This banner, paired with the simple home page layout and friendly, calming color palette, is intended to help users into the right headspace for grocery shopping.

To solve the obstacle of visual clutter and overwhelm, the number of images on the website was scaled back. This way, each time an image is featured on the site, it has a specific purpose. For example, the quirky black and white illustrations were repurposed to draw attention to special announcements or important user decision points. The black and white illustrations work to support the text rather than pulling focus or creating confusion.
